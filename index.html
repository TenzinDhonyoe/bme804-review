Microactuators

An actuator converts electrical, optical, magnetic, or chemical energy into mechanical work — state variables are displacement, force, and velocity. In BioMEMS the same energies directly drive microfluidic valves and pumps, position detectors, and dispense medications.

Specifications — what to spec on every actuator

  • Output force & displacement range
  • Response time
  • Energy / power
  • Stiffness, damping
  • Accuracy (% of displacement or force)
  • Linearity, sensitivity
  • Size, materials & fabrication
  • Repeatability, durability, reliability
Energy density $E_a=W/V_m=F\cdot d/V_m$ (J/m³). Scale: electrostatic 10³ → magnetic 10⁴ → bimetallic 10⁵ → thermopneumatic 10⁶ → piezoelectric & SMA 10⁷.

Electrostatic

Opposite charges on two plates attract → displacement. Basic capacitor physics.

$$F_x^{+}=Q^{+}\tfrac{E}{2}=\tfrac{1}{2}Q\tfrac{V}{x}=\tfrac{1}{2}\tfrac{CV^{2}}{x}=\tfrac{1}{2}\tfrac{\varepsilon_0\varepsilon_r A}{x^{2}}V^{2}$$
  • Force scales with V², area A, and 1/x².
  • Pull-in voltage: above this the movable plate snaps onto the fixed plate and sticks.
  • Comb-drive — interdigitated fingers, in-plane motion (micro-tweezers).
  • Micromirror — serpentine springs, out-of-plane motion (light-beam steering).

What physically happens above the pull-in voltage, and why?
Click to reveal
The deflection exceeds one-third of the gap and the restoring spring can no longer balance the electrostatic force, so the movable plate collapses onto the fixed plate and sticks. Below Vpi the system is stable; above, it's runaway.

Piezoelectric

Transduction between mechanical and electrical signals. Charge ↔ strain. Works as sensor or actuator.

$$d_{x,y}=\frac{Q}{\sigma}=\frac{S}{E}\qquad S=d_{x,y}\cdot E=d_{x,y}\cdot\frac{V}{z}$$
  • Direct effect: stress → charge/voltage (sensing).
  • Converse effect: voltage → strain (actuating).
  • Strain ∝ electric field; $d$ is a material property in C/N.

Direct vs converse piezoelectric effect — which is used for sensing, which for actuation, and why?
Click to reveal
Direct effect = mechanical stress → charge/voltage → sensing. Converse effect = applied voltage → strain → actuation. Same crystal, same $d$ constant, two directions. That's why a PMUT can both transmit and receive ultrasound.

Electromagnetic

Current-carrying conductor in a B-field feels a Lorentz force; ferromagnetic cantilever deflects under magnetic field.

$$F=I\,\ell\sin\vartheta\cdot B,\qquad B=\frac{\mu_0\mu_r N_i I}{\ell}$$
  • Displacement X ∝ B-field.
  • Needs a solenoid coil (N turns) and a ferromagnetic beam/target.
Soft microrobots. Silicone (Ecoflex) + NdFeB hard-magnetic microparticles (~5 µm). Time-varying external field → multimodal locomotion (rolling/walking). Hu, Nature Letters 2018.

Electroactive Polymers (EAPs)

Soft actuators that change shape in response to an electric field. Used for artificial muscles, bionics, and micro/nanorobots.

  • Electronic EAPs
    • Dielectric Elastomer Actuators (DEAs) — elastomer film between compliant electrodes; E-field creates electrostatic pressure that squeezes the polymer, which expands in the planar direction.
    • Ferroelectric polymers.
  • Ionic EAPs (IPMCs) — two metal electrodes either side of an ion-polymer film filled with electrolyte. E-field drives cations toward one plate; cations drag solvent molecules → film bends (biomorph bending).
DEA vs IPMC — which is ionic, what drives the motion, and what kind of motion do you get?
Click to reveal
DEA is electronic — electrostatic pressure squeezes a dielectric elastomer between compliant electrodes; the film expands in the planar direction. IPMC is ionic — an electric field drives cations through an electrolyte-filled polymer; the cations drag solvent, one side swells, and the strip bends (biomorph).

Thermal, SMAs and shape-memory polymers

  • SMAs — thermally-induced phase change restores a pre-programmed shape.
  • SMPs — switching segments fix "shape B" after extension & cooling; heating above $T_\text{trans}$ relaxes them back. Also light-, electrically-, or environmentally-triggered. High elastic deformation; shape-memory hydrogels are used in biomedicine.
  • Thermal transformation — bimetallic bending, thermopneumatic expansion.

Application — drug delivery

  • Passive — no energy (diffusion). Active — energy-powered dispensing.
  • Routes: transdermal, subdermal, implanted.
  • Components: reservoirs, microvalves, micropumps, microchannels, ports, microsensors.
  • Apps: pain control, diabetes, ocular disease. Considerations: site, volume, refilling.
Microneedles. Transdermal; Si/glass/polymer/PDMS molding. Types: hollow, coated, biodegradable.
Passive ocular device. Refillable reservoir; delivery when $P_\text{reservoir}\!>\!P_\text{cannula}$; check valve; 2× PDMS layers; 22.5 µL.
Electrolytic pump. RF-controlled electrolysis; bubbles expand the chamber and push drug through the cannula.
Piezoelectric pump. Balloon reservoir + PEA driving microchannels via an external coil (wireless).
Multi-reservoir implant. Anode caps dissolve on demand to expose individual drug wells (Sutradhar 2016).
Hydrogels. pH/glucose-sensitive; swell/shrink to occlude or open ports through an etched Si membrane.

Biosensors & Lab-on-a-Chip

A biosensor is the interface between an analyte and a transducer. Apps: diabetes and other chronic conditions, cancer & cardiovascular biomarkers, infectious disease, genetics, sweat/performance tracking.

Biosensor anatomy

  • Three parts: recognition element + transducer + amplifier.
  • Recognition: enzymes, antibodies, nucleic acids, aptamers.
  • Immunoassays: ELISA, microfluidic, lab-on-a-chip.
  • Transducers: acoustic, piezoelectric, electrical, electromechanical, optical, thermal, mechanical.
Bioreceptors (catalytic / affinity) × Immobilization (physical / chemical) × Transduction (optical / electrochemical) — three axes that define a biosensor.

Catalytic (enzyme-based) vs non-enzymatic

  • Catalytic — enzymes (e.g. oxidases) accelerate reactions; immobilized on the transducer. Mediators like ferrocene speed the reaction and regenerate the enzyme. Highly specific/sensitive but fragile (T and pH).
  • Non-enzymatic — direct adsorption. Lower cost, more stable.
    • Noble metals (Pt, Ni, Ag, Au) — corrosion-resistant; issues: poisoning, pH, biofouling, cost.
    • Metal oxides (Co, Cu, Zn) — cheaper.
    • Carbon nanomaterials (graphene, CNTs, nanowires) — high electron transfer.
    • Molecularly imprinted polymers (MIPs) — hydrogels.
What does a mediator (e.g. ferrocene) actually do in an enzyme-based biosensor?
Click to reveal
It shuttles electrons between the enzyme's active site and the electrode, speeding up the reaction and regenerating the enzyme (returning it to its active form). Without a mediator, many enzyme reactions are too slow to give a useful current.

Antibodies & aptamers

Immunosensor signal = change in mass / impedance / optical intensity from antigen–antibody binding.

  • Antibodies bind antigens through weak, non-covalent forces — van der Waals, electrostatic, hydrogen bonds.
  • Common IgG, IgM, IgA, IgE.
  • Immobilized by physical adsorption or covalent bonds. Challenges: T and pH sensitivity in Ab production.
  • Aptamers are short single-stranded DNA/RNA synthesized via SELEX (pool → incubate → select → elute → amplify → repeat).

Which ELISA is best for small antigens at low concentration, and why is sandwich not a good fit?
Click to reveal
Competitive ELISA wins for small antigens at low concentration — the sample antigen competes with a bound antigen for limited Ab binding sites, so even tiny amounts show up as a measurable decrease in signal. Sandwich fails here because the antigen must be large enough for two different antibodies to bind two different epitopes simultaneously.

Immobilization

  • Physical (reversible) — adsorption (vdW, electrostatic, ionic, H-bonds), entrapment (polymerization), encapsulation (semi-permeable shell).
  • Chemical (irreversible) — covalent bonding to the surface, or cross-linking into aggregates that bind the surface.

Transduction methods

Electrical

  • Amperometry, cyclic voltammetry, potentiometry, impedance.
  • ISFETs — ion-sensitive gate; enzymatic reaction modulates source-drain current.

Optical

  • Fluorescence — fluorophores (QDs, fluorescent proteins); NIR preferred.
  • Chemiluminescence — substrate + enzyme → photon emission (no excitation source).
  • Colorimetry — color change via absorbance.
  • SAW — acoustic wave on piezo substrate; velocity depends on mass loading.
  • SPR — incident light excites plasmons at metal/dielectric interface; intensity tracks molecular interactions at the surface.

Lab-on-a-chip / µTAS

Whole wet-lab workflow on one chip: sampling → pre-treatment → mixing → reaction → separation → detection → signal processing.

  • Benefits: multiple functionalities, simultaneous multi-analyte analysis, high-throughput with small sample volumes, portable POC, automation.
  • Apps: DNA/protein sequencing, POC, blood analysis, organs-on-chip, cell culture, single-cell/cellomics, drug discovery.
  • Sample-to-answer capabilities: surface functionalization, reagent integration, sample prep, flow control, transducer integration.
Surface modification. Hydrophobic/philic patches, adhesion, active binding agents; traps, DEP cages, wells; prevent biofouling.
Reagent integration. On-chip storage (dry spotted / liquid blisters); dispensing via mechanical blisters or thermal one-shot valves.
Sample prep. Filtration, DEP, acoustophoresis, pinched flow; micromixers; reservoirs meter volumes.
Flow control. Capillary (passive geometry) or active (integrated valves/pumps); drives mixing, washing, incubation.

Microarrays and PCR

  • DNA microarrays. Thousands of known probe sequences; on-chip hybridization. Steps: denature → label (green/red) → bind complementary spots → wash → scan.
  • PCR — invented by Kary Mullis, 1983. Exponential DNA amplification. Two primers (forward/reverse).
    • Three steps: Denaturation → Annealing → Extension.
    • Applications: COVID-19, HIV, Hepatitis B & C.

Point-of-Care Testing

  • Biomarker identification, disposable cartridges, hand-held readout, non- or minimally-invasive, low cost, mobile connectivity.
  • Devices: paper-based microfluidics, lateral-flow, LOC, Lab-on-a-disc (LOAD), microfluidic PCR.
  • Applications: home testing (diabetes), emergency testing, high-risk identification, infectious disease monitoring, low-resource settings.

Microsensors

A sensor is a transducer that converts a measurand into a signal. Stimuli: acoustic, biological, chemical, electrical, magnetic, optical, mechanical, radiation, thermal.

Definitions

  • Sensor = transducer + conditioning. Then ADC → digital processing → DAC → actuator.
  • Classified by measurand, transduction, material, technology, or application.
Transfer characteristic: $S=a+bX$

Specifications

Static

  • Linearity — deviation from straight line.
  • Resolution — smallest detectable input change.
  • Sensitivity — slope $dS/dX$.
  • Selectivity — target / non-target response.
  • Dynamic range, noise (thermal, LOD), offset, hysteresis, impedance, efficiency.

Dynamic

  • Time constant τ — output reaches 63.2% of a step.
  • Settling time — 90% (~3τ).
  • Tolerance δ, noise, drift.

What does the time constant τ physically mean, and how is settling time related?
Click to reveal
τ is the time for the output to reach 63.2% of a step input's final value (first-order system). Settling time (to within ~10%) is approximately 3τ; to within 2% it's ~5τ. Small τ = fast sensor.

Impedimetric — EIS

Low-complexity, low-cost, label-free; measures electrical properties of the interface and medium. Apps: capacitive sensing (cell fingerprinting), impedance flow cytometry, cell growth/contractility, cancer detection, EIT imaging.

$$Z(\omega)=\frac{V(\omega)}{I(\omega)}=\operatorname{Re}\{Z\}+j\operatorname{Im}\{Z\}$$
  • Randles model: $R_s$ (solution), $C_{DL}$ (double layer), $R_{CT}$ (charge transfer), $Z_W$ (Warburg, diffusion).
  • $C_{DL}$ isn't ideal — model as a CPE: $Z_{CPE}=1/[(j\omega)^n C]$, $0\!<\!n\!<\!1$.
  • Impedance flow cytometry. Low f (<1 MHz): ΔZ/Z ∝ cell volume (separate by size). High f: ΔZ/Z ∝ volume + cytoplasm conductivity (separate by cell type).
  • IDE electrodes. Au/Pt/C on Si/glass/ceramic/plastic/paper; protective layers SU-8, PDMS, PMMA.

System Integration — Packaging, Powering, Safety

Partitioning a BioMEMS device into functional subsystems, assembling them, keeping them hermetic, and powering them safely — for lab-on-chip, POC, wearable, and implantable use.

Packaging considerations

  • Overall size, hermeticity (waterproof), light, T/P/humidity, EM fields & radiation, implant environment (body fluids, gases), biocompatibility, mechanical stress, powering.
  • Heterogeneous / modular (disposable / reusable / degradable); 2D / 2.5D / 3D.

Bonding processes

Epoxy (adhesive). Cure at 150°C or UV; bond strength sensitive to T and humidity.
Fusion / direct. Wafer-to-wafer, no intermediate layer; Si–O, Si–N, Si–Si covalent. Needs T > 1000°C and flat surfaces. Hydrophilic (Si–OH + HO–Si → Si–O–Si + H₂O) or hydrophobic (H/F-terminated Si in vacuum). Plasma-activated: 25–400°C.
Anodic. Electrochemical; polarization of glass Na⁺/O⁻; Na⁺ travels to cathode; Si oxidizes to Si–O–Si. 180–500°C, 200–1500 V. Can damage ICs — usually MEMS only.
Eutectic. Metal thin-film intermediate melts below either constituent. Au/Si (81.4/18.6) → 363°C. Also Al/Ge/Si, Au/SiSn, Au/Ge/Si. Industry standard.
Solder. Low T, high strength; interconnects via indium bumps, wire bonding, flip-chip.

Surface forces in wafer bonding

  • van der Waals — $F_v = A/(6\pi d^3)$; Hamaker constant $A$; includes hydrogen bonds (H–O, H–N, H–F).
  • Electrostatic — strongest in ionic materials (glass) → Si–glass anodic bonds.
  • Capillary — contact angle + surface tension.

Wire bonding & flip-chip

  • Wire bond — die glued to substrate; pads connected to tracks via Au wires. Ball-to-wedge or wedge-to-wedge.
  • Flip-chip — solder bumps on die; die flipped onto substrate, flux applied → reflow → pressure + heat. Sequence: fluxing → die placement → reflow → flux cleaning → underfill → cure.
Which bonding method: needs >1000°C and atomically flat surfaces · needs 200–1500 V and Na⁺ glass · melts below either constituent · uses indium bumps?
Click to reveal
Fusion / direct bonding → >1000°C, flat Si surfaces, forms Si–O–Si chemical bonds. Anodic → 200–1500 V + 180–500°C, Na⁺ drifts in glass to cathode, Si oxidizes. Eutectic → metal thin-film intermediate melts below either constituent (Au/Si = 363°C). Solder / flip-chip → indium bumps form interconnects at low T.

Hermetic packaging & encapsulation

  • Moisture-free to avoid polarization, metal corrosion, electrolytic conduction.
  • Protect the device from hazards and protect the biology from device hazards (HV, chemistry, heat).
  • Permeability ranking (low → high): Metals < Glasses < Fluoro-carbons < Epoxies < Silicones.
  • Integrated micromachining — packaging built into the microfab. Device-specific.
  • Post-fabrication packaging — die carriers (plastic/ceramic): rigid, hermetic, reliable.
  • 3D stacking — reduces footprint; through-substrate vias (TSVs) for chip-to-chip; die-to-die / package-to-package / heterogeneous.

Powering & energy harvesting

Passive or active; external or implanted; voltage & current requirements, max/avg power, local heating; batteries vs battery-less (wireless, harvesting, scavenging).

  • Thin-film battery — size, lifetime, replacement, toxicity.
  • Photovoltaic — pn-junction; photons → electrons; low efficiency; max tissue transmittance in NIR.
  • Thermoelectric generators (TEG) — Seebeck across p- and n-type semiconductors; ΔT → voltage; load → current. $\Delta V = \alpha_S \Delta T$.
  • Piezoelectric harvesters — V ∝ strain; cantilever / membrane / PZT fibers in PDMS / ZnO nanowires. PZT has lead; alternatives AlN, ZnO, BaTiO₃.
  • Glucose/O₂ biofuel cell

Why do inductive and ultrasound WPT both work well through tissue, while microwave struggles?
Click to reveal
Inductive coupling uses near-field B-fields at low frequency — tissue is effectively transparent to magnetic fields at kHz–MHz, so penetration is strong and efficiency is high. Ultrasound uses mechanical waves, which propagate well through soft tissue (same reason medical imaging works). Microwave is high-frequency EM radiation — water absorbs it strongly (that's how microwave ovens cook), so most of the energy heats the tissue instead of reaching the implant.

  • IEEE C95.1-2019 — human RF exposure.
  • MPE — Maximum Permissible Exposure (W/cm², measured e.g. over 30 min). Depends on RF frequency.
  • SAR — Specific Absorption Ratio (W/kg); local tissue temperature rise < 1–2°C.

Biosafety & biocompatibility

Do not confuse. Biostability = the material keeps its physical/chemical integrity after implantation. Biocompatibility = the material does not cause harmful biological reactions.
  • Common materials: Ti, Ir, Co, Au, Pt; Si, SiO₂, Si₃N₄, SiC; PDMS, parylene-C, polyimide.
  • Bioresorbable: Mg, Zn, silk fibroin.
  • Inflammatory response.
    • Foreign-body reaction — non-specific clean-up.
    • Specific immune reaction (T-cells) — depends on materials.
  • Fibrotic encapsulation.
    • ✓ Bioinert reaction; implant fixed in position.
    • ✗ Insulation layer alters the electrical properties of the device.
Biostability vs biocompatibility — what's the difference, and why does fibrotic encapsulation have a pro AND a con?
Click to reveal
Biostability = the material stays physically and chemically intact inside the body (doesn't corrode, dissolve, degrade). Biocompatibility = the body doesn't react harmfully to the material. Different failure modes. Fibrotic encapsulation: ✓ bioinert reaction fixes the implant in place (good for stability); ✗ the fibrous capsule is electrically insulating, so recording and stimulation electrodes gradually lose performance.
